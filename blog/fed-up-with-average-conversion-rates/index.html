Don’t Read This Unless You’re Fed Up With Average Conversion Rates

Coy Whittier · May 29, 2015
All Quiz Tips & Strategy Webinars
Shares

A 5% lead conversion rate is a lot like asking your cousin to prom. Sure—it’s better than nothing, but you and I both know it could be a whole lot better.

In this week’s webinar, we sat down with Sushant Sharma of Visual Website Optimizer to discuss how quizzes are driving killer conversion rates. To say it was a helpful conversation would be grossly inadequate. Read on to get a few insights into the process behind 20-30% conversion rates.

As I recently discussed on the Wordstream blog, we recently discovered that Qzzr quizzes ending with lead capture forms were successful at acquiring a lead 10% of the time. Yeah, that’s the average! It only took basic marketing prowess to get above average conversion rates.

What would you do if your conversion rates were that high?

Owen and Sushant shared countless quiz tips (and you really should watch the webinar below to get all of them), but we’ll highlight just three that will get you started:

Know your audience

Quizzes and polls are examples of interactive content, but without knowing who you’re designing the quiz for, there’s not going to be much interaction. Here’s a quick diagram that illustrates precisely the mindset you should be in when designing your quiz.

It doesn’t do you a bit of good to create a quiz that only you would want to take. This seems obvious, but it’s remarkably common. Learn as much as you can about your target personas.

What challenges do they face? What entertains them? What do they want to learn more about? Maybe draw inspiration from the news or pop culture, depending on your audience. Find that happy middle where interaction flows naturally. Turn that prom date from awkward cousin conversation into riveting, engaging dialogue. That’s what spurs an above average conversion rate.

Show me something cool

Quizzes are by nature much more social than other forms of content. They’re personal. So ditch the office-speak and loosen the tie and have a little fun with it.

Remember this quiz from the webinar? Again, pardon the crass title, but it’s not what you think. This is from the College Humor quiz “Whose cleavage is this?”

If you don’t recognize him, this is Dog the Bounty Hunter. While I’m not sure if I would classify his cleavage as “cool,” you get the point. This quiz is engaging because it’s out of the ordinary and combats the overarching assumption.

Here’s another one, since we’re on the bounty hunter theme (no, I’m not going to make any references to the hit 90s bounty hunter-themed TV show Renegade). When Sideshow Collectibles made this quiz: “Which Star Wars bounty hunter are you?”, they could’ve asked simple, straightforward questions. But they saw the magic of a quiz, and created questions like this:

A quiz is not a real assessment. You aren’t confined by the bounds of scientific thought and limits, so again—have some fun with it.

Help me feel sufficiently assessed

I know I just said quizzes aren’t real assessments, but I wasn’t completely serious. They need to retain some degree of legitimacy for people to invest in the result. If your quiz is three questions long, but then you try and offer me insights about who I am, it’s not going to seem legitimate to me and I’ll be significantly less likely to share my results.

The quiz on the left isn’t likely to make me feel confident in the result I get. “Oh, I should buy a Lexus? You asked me three questions, I don’t think you know me well enough to make that kind of call.” However, in the quiz on the right, there are 10 questions to ascertain my personality, preferences, and character. So the fact that I get a Boss 302 makes me want to brag about it to my friends.
