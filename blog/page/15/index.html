  • Quiz Takers Are People Too: How to Discover Your Audience with Quiz Analytics

    John Williams · November 24, 2014

    Interactive quizzes are wildly popular both with the marketers who create them and the people who take them. The entertainment value of quizzes such as “Which Disney Villain are You?” and “Which State Should You Actually Live In?” is high, as is the value to marketers. From the user’s perspective, these quizzes are fun and worth sharing with their friends. From the marketer’s perspective, all that sharing is huge! Best of all, user-friendly quiz building services like QZZR make building a lead generation quiz a simple matter. While using quizzes to generate leads or drive traffic is smart, you can also use quizzes to find out more about your audience and discover risks and opportunities. This requires using quizzes and analytics to gather useful information.

    What Are Your Quiz Takers Struggling With?

    For example, let’s say that you offer accounting and tax services and have a quiz that tests the user’s knowledge about business taxes. As more people take the quiz, you’ll soon find areas where quiz takers consistently struggle to get the answer right. Maybe they’re confused about paying estimated taxes or have misconceptions about certain filing requirements. Whatever it is, if you see a question where a large number of quiz takers are having trouble, you should also see an opportunity to help educate your audience. How? If your audience doesn’t understand their estimated tax payment obligations, you could write an article about estimated tax payments. If your audience is confused about the differences between LLCs, partnerships, sole proprietorships, and S corporations, you could write a downloadable report on the topic or create a PowerPoint presentation that explains the differences.

    How to Figure Out What Your Quiz Takers Are Struggling With

    So, how do you know that they’re confused about estimated taxes or business entity types? Two ways: Framing your questions properly and analytics. This requires work at both the front end and the back end.

    Framing Your Questions

    Sure, you could create a quiz such as “Which TV or Movie Accountant Would You Trust With Your Business Taxes?” and then serve up Rick Moranis (Louis Tully) in Ghostbusters or Oscar Nunez (Oscar Martinez) in The Office, but that’s not what this type of quiz is about. In this case, you want to find out how you can better serve your customers and prospects. That said, you may want to align your questions with your top area of expertise or the products and services you prefer to offer. For example, if you specialize in LLCs, you’ll want to focus your questions on tax issues that affect LLCs.

    One way to quickly find out what your quiz takers know about a given subject is to use myths/misconceptions and a true and false format. For example:

    LLC owners report business income and losses on their personal tax returns.
    a. True
    b. False

    On the hand, if you want to know what their thought processes are, you might try questions with multiple choice answers such as:

    When it appears likely that you’ve underpaid your taxes by a large amount, you:
    a. Immediately file an extension
    b. Panic and think about shutting down the business
    c. Find out how much you will likely owe and then come up with a monthly budget
    d. Fire my accountant

    Using Analytics

    Now that you have your quiz created and published, you’ll use analytics to gather useful information. Assuming your quiz has a call to action (such as an email opt-in or special offer), you’ll want to see how well your quiz is doing from a conversion point of view. QZZR will tell you exactly what your conversion rate is. Simply click on your quiz and then click on Stats to view the results of your quiz.

    However, in this example, you’re not solely interested in conversions. You want to find out how informed your audience really is about your niche. You want to discover risks and opportunities. For example, if your quiz takers unsure about how to report business income for LLCs, that’s an area you can address via blog posts or articles. On the other hand, what if you find out that most of your prospects indicate a likelihood of firing their accountants at the first hint of bad news? That’s a risk you need to know about!

    QZZR’s analytics tool displays each of your questions along with the percentage of correct and incorrect responses. Percentages for correct answers are color-coded green while those for wrong answers are color-coded red.

    As a marketing tool, quizzes are incredibly versatile. Make sure to include at least one quiz in your arsenal that helps you learn more about your audience.

  • Create Fun Online Quizzes With this “Not-So-Secret” Sauce

    John Williams · November 18, 2014

    So, you’ve signed up for a free or pro Qzzr account and are ready to create your first quiz, now what? While the software itself is designed to make creating fun online quizzes super easy, you will still need to write your questions, answers, and results pages. Don’t worry, you don’t need to be a professional writer in order to do this. You will, however, need to put some thought into writing your quiz content. Use the tips below to plan your first first (or next) quiz.

    Define Your Goal for the Quiz – Each quiz should have a specific purpose, and you should be crystal clear as to what that purpose is before you type a single character. Examples of goals for a quiz include: social sharing, lead generation, or presenting a call to action. For instance, if your goal is to encourage social sharing so that your fan page gets more engagement and/or likes, you might want to choose a quiz topic that has the potential to go viral. On the other hand, if your goal is to qualify and generate leads, your topic should be more narrowly focused on your prospective customers’ pain points as they relate to your product or service. It’s virtually impossible to create an effective quiz until you know what you want to accomplish with it.

    Choose a Format for Your Quiz - Once you know what you want to accomplish, choose a format for your quiz. Options include multiple choice questions, true or false questions, or a mix. There’s no right or wrong approach, but you should decide on a format so that you can start brainstorming questions.

    Develop Your Results First – Did you know that many authors write their books’ endings before the beginning? This helps them to know where they are going with a story. The same concept can be applied to quiz writing. For example, if you know that your results will consist of four answers — you’re an IT rockstar; you’re a computer geek; you’re a nerd-in-the-making; and you’re a good candidate for IT training school — you’ll be well on your way to developing questions and answers that ultimately lead to those four results. In this example, not only will you need to ask questions that test your quiz takers’ knowledge on various IT and computer topics, you’ll need to develop answers appropriate for each result. In order to be an IT rockstar, quiz takers will need to answer difficult questions correctly and consistently.

    Develop Characteristics That Match Your Results - If your results involve some sort of persona, such as an IT rockstar, king of office supplies, or humble servant, try to make some of your quiz answers reflect those characteristics. For example, answers that the “humble servant” would be likely to answer might have a polite tone. When the time comes, try to keep each persona in mind as you develop your questions and answers.

    Write Your Title to Reflect Your Results - Now that you know the ending and have a sense of the various personas involved, write a quiz title that reflects your results. In our earlier example, a good quiz title might be, “What kind of a computer nerd are you?” The answer: either an IT rockstar, computer geek, nerd-in-the-making, or IT training school candidate. See how that works? Of course, this can work both ways. If you’ve already decided it would be to have a quiz titled “What kind of an office manager are you,” you can just as easily develop your final results. Either way, try to define your ultimate results before you begin writing your questions and answers.

    Write Your Questions and Answers - At last, it’s time to write your questions and answers. As you come up with questions, try to come up with one answer from each end-result. When you enter your answers in the QZZR user interface, you’ll see a dropdown box next to the answer field where you’ll associate each answer with the appropriate end-result.

    Respect your quiz takers’ time – Keep your quizzes short and fast-paced. Social quizzes are not meant to be long, painstaking projects. If your quiz is deemed too hard, boring, or time-consuming, quiz takers will not complete it let alone take the action that you want them to take at the end.

    Finally, keep the goal you defined earlier in mind as you write your quiz. This will help you stay on track. For example, if your goal is to generate leads, your questions and answers should signal to quiz takers that you know what you’re talking about. They should help to build your credibility. If your goal is to encourage social sharing, incorporate viral elements. In both cases, your quizzes should be engaging and fun. After all, quizzes are meant to be a creative way to interact with website visitors and social media followers.

  • Create a Quiz Campaign Like LucidChart’s by Choosing the Right Topics

    Bucky Flowers · November 10, 2014

    This post is part of a series documenting our experiment with using quizzes for B2B marketing. Last week we talked about our decision to try out quizzes for our online marketing approach. This week we discuss how we built our campaign around this new technology.

    At Lucidchart we live and breathe diagrams. Our company operates under a SaaS model targeting different personas: IT/Engineering, Product Management and Design, and Business. We offer both free accounts and paid Pro subscriptions for our diagramming tool. Our goal as a marketing department is to build awareness for our product and convince people to pay for the Pro version after trying it for free.

    So we decided to create a quiz campaign with topics that would easily fit into our existing marketing funnel.

    Brainstorming

    To best determine what those quizzes should be, we started with a rapid-fire brainstorming session, keeping the funnel in mind. We brainstormed ideas that would appeal to both existing and potential customers. At this stage, even silly ideas were welcomed since they helped us approach the task with an open mind.

    We eliminated topics by asking ourselves key questions: where in the funnel would this quiz apply? Which areas of the funnel did we want to target? We organized our brainstormed topics around the marketing funnel, then the selected topics (highlighted in red below) we thought would move customers down the funnel in clear, measurable ways. Keep in mind that rejected topics may still have potential marketing value, so it’s a good idea to save your work.

    Product Awareness

    Our first quiz set matches up with the top of the marketing funnel. It focuses on creating awareness, interest, and consideration for our product. We decided to use “Are you an expert in…?” style quizzes that applied to diagrams, e.g. “Are you an expert in entity-relationship diagrams?” or “How well do you know flowcharts?” We are limiting this set to diagram-types that we know have a high rate of ROI, like flowcharts and org charts. These types of quizzes are popular online right now because, let’s face it, people are vain. They like to post their quiz results on social media to let everyone know how smart they are.

    To advertise this series of quizzes, we’ll be using targeted promoted tweets. We’ve had success with promoted tweets leading to high click-through rates before, and we’re comfortable running PPC campaigns. After they take our quiz, readers will be prompted to provide their email address for more quizzes, diagramming tips, and info about diagramming software from Lucidchart. Since anyone who clicks on the quiz is likely to be a frequent diagrammer, they’ll be more inclined to sign up.

    Product Purchasing

    Our second quiz set was targeted to users farther down the marketing funnel, under intent, evaluation, and purchase. We have a large base of unpaid users that we want to a.) retain and b.) eventually convert to paid users. So we are planning to send an email blast out to unpaid users with a quiz titled “What kind of diagram should I use for my next project?”

    We chose this topic because it shamelessly shows off Lucidchart’s best features, including those that many users may not be aware of (e.g. ER diagrams). Additionally, if the quiz taker’s answers result in a diagram type that’s included in our Pro version, we can invite them to purchase a Pro account. We’ll even have a convenient discount offer right at the end of the quiz. We are always looking to convert our users to paid subscriptions without being too pushy, and we think this is a perfect way to do that.

    Now that we’ve determined our topics and strategy, it’s time to write and post our quizzes. Depending on how things go, we may need to update our strategy or tweak our topics—so we’ll keep you posted here on our continued progress. For now, we’re excited to finally be diving into Qzzr’s software!
