  • Goal! 5 Sports Quizzes That Deposit the Scoring Device in the Receptacle

    Coy Whittier · April 8, 2015

    You like sports, right?

    Well, if you do, you’ll be interested in these quizzes. If not, you may still get a lot out of them. They certainly seem to attract more fans than One Direction at a middle school assembly. Combined, they have been taken nearly half a million times.

    In no particular order, here are five of our top sports quizzes.

    Sports Illustrated: What Would Bill Belichick Say?

    If you’ve watched any interviews with Bill Belichick, you’ll notice that the man keeps things close to the vest. Interviews with him tend to be – how should we say – less than revealing.


    espnW: What World Cup Team Are You?

    Yes, the World Cup was a year ago. No, we aren’t going to let that get in the way of finding out which team we most identify with. One piece of advice: open yourself up to the possibility that it may not be who you think it will.

    EuroSport: Quelle Star du Foot Êtes-Vous?

    Google Translate and three months in French-speaking Africa helped me figure out that this quiz is asking “Which Football Star Are You?” and that it’s been taken over 250,000 times. Aside from that, I’m lost.

    One interesting thing about this – over the past few months, we have been working hard to translate the Qzzr experience into foreign languages, including Spanish, French, Chinese, and more. This means it’s easier and easier for people throughout the world to create simple, elegant quizzes to drive social traffic and generate leads. Look for more on this in coming weeks.

    Squakwa: Which Top Football Manager Are You?

    Spoiler alert: We’re not bringing Belichick back into the mix. This quiz relates to the football that the other 95% of the world plays.

    College Humor: NFL Player or Key & Peele Character?

    College Humor is always good for a funny quiz, and this one does not disappoint. While not directly sports-related, it does shine a humorous light on some of the harder-to-pronounce players’ names in the NFL.

    No matter the subject, it’s clear that quizzes have the ability to engage an audience. Whether your goal is to increase your brand’s social engagement on Facebook or increase the amount of fun at your high school reunion, a quiz might be just the thing for you.   • The Qzzr Guide to Out-of-This-World Ecommerce Conversion Rates

    Coy Whittier · April 7, 2015

    Don’t get me wrong. I’m all for determining which Frozen character I am, or finding out how well I know Armenian pop songs from the 80s. In fact, I consider these basic human needs—equal in importance to food, water, and shelter.

    Nonetheless, the value of quizzes can extend past this. In particular, many top brands have been realizing incredible ecommerce conversion rates by incorporating quizzes into their content strategies.

    How do they do this? Like any good marketing effort, it begins with a goal. Of course, while the ultimate objective of any ecommerce company is found in the latter part of that word—commerce—a successful company will break this down into smaller, more easily measured and influenced metrics. Examples may be:

    • Brand awareness/recognition
    • Web traffic
    • Email subscriptions
    • Data acquisition
    • Revenue
    • Decreased bounce rate
    • Improved organic search

    Let’s take a look at five brands in particular, and how quizzes are helping them bring home the bacon.

    Zenni Optical

    We’ve spoken about these guys before, but when it comes to ecommerce success, they’re the crown jewel. To date, their You’ve Been Framed quiz has been taken over 415,000 times, generated over 23,000 leads, and has generated nearly $400,000 in revenue. And the best part is that the thing’s a friggin’ conifer – as evergreen as green can be. They’ve long stopped promoting it, but it keeps churning.

    We’ll give this some more detail as we go along, but one of the secrets to this quiz’s success is its role as a decision engine. It takes a difficult scenario – selecting the right eyeglass frame for your style, features, activity level, etc. – and asks simple questions to determine the best fit. True – they could’ve spelled it all out in a longform blog post or infographic, but they didn’t. The quiz draws people in and helps resolve one of the top obstacles causing bounce rate. This in turn drives revenue. And the result has been astounding.

    Update 6/2/15: This quiz has now generated more than 31,000 new leads and over $1 million in revenue. Incredible!

    Sideshow Collectibles

    When figurine studio Sideshow Collectibles was faced with the challenge of launching a new lineup, they created a quiz to sort fans into various factions integral to the overarching story, Court of the Dead. At the end of the quiz, people were presented with a coupon and an opportunity to win a limited edition figurine from the collection.

    The result was astounding. Of the nearly 37,000 people that took the quiz, Sideshow Collectibles generated over 22,000 leads. That’s a 60% ecommerce conversion rate(!), which as of October had directly prompted over 1,800 orders and more than $75,000 in sales.

    As if that was not enough, by using the questions they did, they had acquired invaluable amounts of data about those subscribers in a way that would allow targeted marketing automation down the road. As far as quiz strategy goes, this case study is the cream of the crop.

    Lime Ricki

    Like Zenni Optical, this modest swimwear brand is using the power or quizzes as a decision engine. By addressing and eliminating one of the largest obstacles in their customers’ minds – indecision or self-consciousness about the style or fit of a swimsuit – they’ve increased web traffic, sales and email subscriptions.

    In addition, the success of this quiz proves potential for quizzes to serve a sales/support role for ecommerce brands. Instead of occupying a salesperson’s time to get recommendations, the quiz is able to direct them to products that fit their body type, interests, color preferences, and more.

    Kettlebell Fever

    It can be easy to get overwhelmed by stories of the big brands, with marketing campaigns blowing past 300,000 leads or more. But it’s important to note that the concept remains the same for small businesses. A perfect example of this is a fitness company called Kettlebell Fever. Their quiz has been taken 549 times, bringing in 100 leads. An 18% ecommerce conversion rate was a big deal for them, and ended up being a nice revenue tick.

    Pampadour

    Another great example of this came from Pampadour, a Pinterest-style network for those wanting to share beauty products and techniques. In trying to expand their blog’s reach, they launched a quiz—paired with a giveaway—that netted them 163 leads, with a 52% conversion rate. That goes a long way in the early days of a blog, as does landing customer data that will allow systematic, interest-based marketing automation.

    Wrap-Up

    In the world of marketing, few tactics bring in those conversion rates. You may be skeptical (and I was, too), but give it a try and see what happens.

  • The Good, the Fab and the Bloody: A Look at 3 Great Quizzes

    Coy Whittier · April 6, 2015

    Occasionally, we like to take a look back a few great quizzes. Some you’ll shake your head at, some you’ll be inspired by, and some you may just learn something from. Take the quizzes to see how well you do. We’ll add some insights from each as needed, but feel free to join the discussion in the comments below.

    The Good: The Michael J. Fox Foundation

    This is a perfect example of a quiz designed to educate and mobilize. In it, you’re presented with several facts and misperceptions about Parkinson’s disease. After answering the last question, boom! – you’re given the option to subscribe to their email list.

    Quizzes are a great way to disseminate information to a large group of people in a friendly, comfortable way. The transition from quiz to ask is nearly seamless. The reader (likely affected in some way by Parkinson’s disease) is introduced to the quiz and offered a chance to learn more. Perfect bump, set and spike.

    The Fab: House Beautiful

    I really liked the mix of image and text-based questions. It’s good to use a combination of both to strike a balance for your audience, but when you’re a home décor publication, you can use all the pictures you want.

    The Bloody: Zap2it

    If you’ve seen the show, you’re a rabid fan. The only thing missing in your life has been a quiz to tell you where you fit in the mix. Until now, that is. Zap2it fulfills this need with a great outcome quiz.

    The scenario-based questions in this quiz are well thought out and clear. I’m asked how I would react in given circumstances, and assigned a character based on my selections.

    Conclusion

    All three of these quizzes have compelling elements. Plug a few in to your quiz strategy and start testing.

    Oh – and in case you’re wondering, I got Tyrion. It obviously does not ask for your height.

  • Why Quizzes? Confessions of a Marketer Not Yet Drunk on Quiz Koolaid

    Coy Whittier · April 3, 2015

    DISCLAIMER: I am the newest Qzzr employee.

    To you, this means two things. One, I’m not about to confess everything. I mean I just unpacked my stuff – I’d be gone before I finished talking about high school. Two, it means I’m not entirely void of bias.

    But what this does give me is unique insight into the world of quizzes, and why they should be incorporated into a robust content strategy.

    As an avid content consumer as well, I recently re-read The 7 Business Goals of Content Marketing from Content Marketing Institute. In so doing, I had a real Gordon Bombay moment and revisited why I’m doing what I’m doing. On an unrelated note, I swear someone was cutting onions next to me at the exact same time …weird.

    In Joe’s post, he outlines the 7 reasons content marketing exists:

    1. Brand awareness or reinforcement
    2. Lead conversion and nurturing
    3. Customer conversion
    4. Customer service
    5. Customer loyalty/retention
    6. Customer upsell
    7. Passionate subscribers

    So, being relatively recently introduced to the power of quizzes (as have you, I’m sure), I began to look at them through the lens of these seven goals.

    Brand Awareness or Reinforcement

    The thing about a good quiz is that it’s engaging. As anyone who has visited social media in the past six months can attest, these things are everywhere. NewsWhip found that nine of the top ten most shared posts on Facebook in January 2015 were quizzes, and Qzzr has found from their (…sorry, our) research that the average quiz gets shared 1,900 times.

    Clearly, there’s something here, right? Shares mean impressions, and impressions mean brand awareness. From what we’re seeing, there does not appear to be a better way of organically spreading your content than letting thousands – even hundreds of thousands – of your followers spread it for you.

    Lead Conversion and Nurturing

    But awareness isn’t everything, right? Once you’re in front of the swarm of potential customers, you have an incredible opportunity to act – to acquire. This window into their time allows you to present a call to action – be it what it may – that will allow you to sign them up, give them more information, or achieve whatever primary goal you happen to have. A recent article on eConsultancy stated that quizzes have an average opt-in rate of 50%.

    Customer Conversion

    Why not just present an offer for purchase? Josh Little, Qzzr’s CEO, created a quick quiz to illustrate the power that a quiz has to drive sales.

    The quiz taker is presented (non-annoyingly) with an offer to add $1,000 to their trade-in value immediately before receiving their results. Offers like this fuel an average of 5% conversion rate in the quizzes we’ve tested – and often much higher. Again, that’s impressive.

    Customer Service

    Surveys have traditionally occupied this space, using a less than subtle and “tale-as-old-as-time” tactic: I ask you, and you tell me. The problem with this format is that it will always smack of “favor” and “inconvenience.” You have to hound – even bribe – people to get responses to surveys.

    With quizzes, you can recover the same information, but people will throw it at you faster than a game of dodgeball. This happens because they are involved, or engaged – exactly the way organizations and educators are using games (not necessarily “gamification”) to motivate and teach. The mentality switches from “How fast can I get this over with?” to “I can’t wait to get my results.” It takes more creativity and planning than puking out 10 questions and flinging them at customers, but it can be done.

    Customer Loyalty and Retention

    For every brand that I have marketed for, there are probably 50 or more brands that I am a customer of – and I’m sure you’re the same. Like me, they probably hit you up endlessly for feedback, engagement, and training. And if I had time to read them, all of those emails probably have every answer to every question I could ever ask and more. But as I described above, when customer outreach transitions from being one-sided and intrusive to collaborative (and dare I say entertaining?!), customers don’t feel used and overwhelmed by the constant barrage. You get people that are more involved and invested in your communications with them.

    Customer Upsell

    Imagine two scenarios for me:

    1. On October 27, you get a text-only email, touting the benefits of new features X and Y, and how much more productive you could be if you upgrade now. And wait – if you do so before the end of the month, you’ll receive 25% off the next three months!
    2. Immediately after interacting with the support team from your lead scoring software, you get an email that includes a quiz. It contains ten questions: eight that test your knowledge of the specific processes you were struggling with, and two that introduce features you don’t currently subscribe to, but that would automate those processes – thus eliminating the need to pull your hair out. After finishing the quiz, you get a popup offering 25% off this new feature for the next three months.

    Which seems like a more positive customer interaction? Which would make you more likely to upgrade?

    Passionate Subscribers

    So this is where I step back and try and look at the whole picture with new eyes. Would incorporating quizzes into lead and customer touches be more or less likely to turn me into a “Passionate Subscriber”? And looking at these benefits, I have to side with quizzes.

    Obviously, your content strategy – like your investment portfolio – should be diverse. Like with anything, customers could get burnt out on quizzes if used improperly or overwhelmingly. While extensive research on interactive content is still coming out to fill in the gaps, existing reports show incredible engagement rates, unmatched virality, and great opportunities for data acquisition.

    Before I wrap up, I have to point out how warm it’s gotten in here. Bring on the Koolaid Man.

  • Graded or Outcome? Tap Into the Psychology of Quiz Types

    Coy Whittier · April 1, 2015

    High-performing quizzes don’t just happen; they come as a result of great content strategy and forethought. One essential element to consider when mapping your quiz action plan is whether your goals will be best met with a graded or outcome quiz.

    Believe it or not, this can hugely influence how your audience will react to the quiz. So just like with any content marketing strategy, it’s imperative that you know both who your target audience is and what they’re aching to get out of any interaction.

    Graded quizzes

    A graded quiz is exactly that – graded. There are right and wrong answers, and at the end of your quiz, the person will know exactly how they did, be it well or – how should we say this? – less than ideal. These quizzes feed the need for competition or challenge, so serve one up if the subject matter is clear and straightforward.

    For example, here’s a quiz that owns exactly what it is – it challenges the reader’s assumption that they know a lot about cars.

    Other ways to make a graded quiz work for you: (click for an example)

    • Can you guess _____?
    • Are you a _______ expert?
    • Can you pass the ____ test?
    • ________ or __________?
    • The world’s hardest ______ quiz.

    Outcome quiz

    You may know these as Personality Quizzes, and will certainly recognize this type if you spend a lot of time on BuzzFeed. In an outcome quiz, there is no right or wrong answer. The person taking the quiz is looking for you to tell them something about themselves that they do not already know. Whereas graded quizzes provide a challenge, outcome quizzes provide intrigue. The person is drawn to finish by their desire to know the – you guessed it – outcome.

    A great outcome quiz was built by swimsuit company Lime Ricki to help customers find a swimsuit to meet their lifestyle and body type.

    One of these other outcome quiz formats may be just what you’re looking for:

    • Which ___ are you?
    • Which ________ is your ________?
    • What kind of ____ are you?
    • Which ____ should you ____?

    Now, once you understand the differences between the two styles, you can start to see how both will fit into your strategy. Some quizzes may just be fun and lighthearted, while others will drive a clear buying signal.
