Independence Day is this weekend and—between backyard barbecues and firework shows—many individuals will be sitting back and enjoying some free time. And what do we do when we’ve got an afternoon to waste? We get on the Internet.

You will see all walks of life posting to your favorite social media channel during the Fourth of July weekend. Between uploading family photos and researching the perfect fried chicken recipe, everyone is bound to skim through Facebook to peek into the lives of everyone else they know.

Capitalize on this social media mayhem by posting the perfect, engaging US history quiz. You will need to find a balance between holiday fun and industry relevance, but if you walk that line just right, your engagement is sure to spike.

Need some help getting started? Here is a list of conversation-starting, thought-provoking Independence Day quiz ideas that will capture your proud customers on everyone’s favorite summer weekend!

1. How Much Do You Know About US History in Pop Culture?

Everyone loves a bit of pop-culture trivia. Capture audiences young and old with this engaging quiz idea where everyone can participate. Where does Independence Day appear in iconic films? Which books have used Independence Day in the plot? Get families talking about their favorite cultural stars and recommending must-see movies after a few fun questions. This taxing US history quiz is bound to dominate social media with its shareable facts and goading headline.

Question Suggestion:

Q: Which Star Trek character quotes the Preamble in episode 52 of the original series, “The Omega Glory?” A: Captain Kirk

2. Which Founding Father Are You?

This quiz headline isn’t particularly unique, but it is catchy. In fact, as I was researching this quiz idea, I found myself taking one of the many founding father quizzes out there. In case you were wondering, I’m Benjamin Franklin.

Sometimes, a quiz doesn’t need to be one-of-a-kind, it just needs to be memorable and timely. And what is more relevant and shareable than a personality quiz that makes people feel proud to be an American?

Question Suggestion:

One question I thought was funny from the quiz I took was:

Q: How tall are you?

  1. Fairly tall
  2. Fairly short
  3. I’m too old to care
  4. I’m too busy fighting duels to measure

(Take the quiz and share your results with us in the comments!)

3. Is This Tradition From the United States or Britain?

Inspire the proud American in everyone on Independence Day with a quiz that highlights the differences between the United States and our motherland. You can look at how the two countries have changed, the silly laws we kept and changed over the years, the differences in holiday traditions, and any other funny facts that will interest quiz takers. Here are some examples.

Question Suggestion:

Q: Which country participates in “worm charming” to collect bait for fishing? A: Britain: The competition was initiated by local man Tom Shufflebotham who on July 5, 1980, charmed 511 worms from the ground in only half an hour. The game has been held every year since as a world championship. The competition has 18 rules. Music of any kind can be used to charm worms out of the ground, but no drugs can be used. Water is considered to be a drug.

Q: Which country uses more pumpkins to celebrate Halloween each year? A: The United States: Illinois alone uses around 500 million a year, the UK uses about 4 million a year in total.

4. How Many Americans Are Actually American?

Ever wondered how American you really are? The United States has long been dubbed a melting pot for different cultures, and the actual breakdown of where today’s population originated from is interesting, and sometimes shocking. Remind quiz takers of their origins and perhaps inspire people to appreciate their culture and this great country in which we live.

Question Suggestion:

Q: True or False? Research has found that more than 6 million Americans who self-identify as European likely carry African ancestry. A: True: A study published in the American Journal of Human Genetics found this to be true, and that as many as 5 million self-described European Americans might have at least 1 percent Native American ancestry as well.

5. Lies US History Taught Us: Are You Smarter Than James Loewen?

Want a little controversy? This time of year, we like to pull out facts from our arsenal of American history—but how much history do we really know? The bestselling book written by James Loewen in 1995, called Lies My Teacher Told Me, outlines a number of inaccuracies in our most popular history textbooks, particularly about the settling of the United States and the idealization of national heroes. Test your followers’ US history knowledge with interesting facts and ideas inspired by Loewen. You’ll give quiz takers some food for thought and establish yourself as an edgy, knowledgeable source.

Question Suggestion:

Q: According to Lies My Teacher Told Me, how much more do American clothing workers make than their Malaysian counterparts? A: 1,500 times more

Conclusion

Holidays are a great time to take advantage of the social engagement that quizzes offer. Take advantage of your potential customers’ free time and grab their attention with something funny, interesting or controversial, but always shareable.

Happy Independence Day!

]]> If you too have been infected by the TV show Suits, you’ll recognize these all-too-common progressive symptoms:

  • Watch one episode of Suits
  • Obsess about Suits
  • Develop unsettling, yet secure man/woman-crush on Harvey Specter
  • Glance at your watch to realize it’s 4AM and you have a meeting in four hours
  • Watch one more episode

If you’ve caught much of the show, you’ll know that Harvey is the kind of guy that says the exact right thing at the exact right time.

So I got to thinking, isn’t that exactly what I try to do as a content creator? And isn’t that commonality enough to create a lasting friendship that could include regular recreational activities together, like—I don’t know—driving cool cars, trying on expensive suits, messing with Louis…

FOCUS, Coy! Sorry, back to my point. Harvey got where he is for a reason (and no, that’s not just because someone wrote that into a script). So I pulled the ten best Harvey Specter quotes and packaged them here for your content marketing enlightenment.

The Top 10 Best Harvey Specter Quotes on Content Marketing

“I don’t pave the way for people… People pave the way for me.”

Success and innovation almost always go together, so move early and move fast. If you’re standing still, you’ll likely get passed over or lost in the fray. There’s a finite amount of attention out there, so only the most unique, best produced content even has a shot at being seen. Is Harvey the kind of guy that would throw together a sloppy blog post? Are you serious? I oughta slap you for even considering it.

“I don’t play the odds, I play the man.”

You didn’t know Harvey was into personalized marketing, did you? If there’s one thing you know about Harvey and Mike Ross, it’s that every situation and case is tirelessly researched. They don’t go into any scenario blindly (with a few disastrous exceptions), and eventually it’s a silver bullet of uncovered information that makes the difference.

Find out what makes your customer tick, then give them what they want—even if they themselves don’t know it yet.

“That’s the difference between you and me—you wanna lose small, I wanna win big”

Win big by finding out what works and then go in for the jugular. How do you do this? Research and testing, my friend.

Do those take time? Absolutely. Are they pleasant? Not always. But do they pay off? In spades. One example showed that testing even just the copy on a button resulted in a 123% improvement on clickthrough rate. I’d call that winning big, wouldn’t you?

“It’s not bragging if it’s true.”

Stop waiting for other people to talk about your successes—it may not happen. Potential customers need to know why they should give their time and money to you, and if they can’t find a reason, they’re gone. If you haven’t noticed, Harvey is not afraid to point out the fact that he’s the best, and I can’t remember one example of a client leaving to another firm (and staying there—if I’m wrong, let me know in the comments).

Now, that doesn’t mean you have to be insufferable about it, as Harvey occasionally is. While confidence and bravado are celebrated characteristics in high-powered NYC corporate law, you may not be able to get away with it in your space. Tout your strengths and showcase your wins, but do so tactfully.

And what’s even better than shouting your own praises? Having someone else tout them for you. A pleased-as-punch customer of ours recently wrote a guest post on the Uberflip blog, detailing the quiz they created, how they promoted it, and how it has generated over $1.1 million in revenue.

“Loyalty is a two way street. If I’m asking for it from you, then you are getting it from me.”

Love this one. It speaks to the symbiotic nature of companies and customers. As marketers, there is no greater goal than earning loyal customers—the kind that keep coming back time and again. However, as consumers, we value the types of businesses that listen and move to meet customer requests. It’s a complex dance in which neither party can become complacent or stationary, and every touch point is a new opportunity to strengthen or weaken that relationship.

“I don’t have dreams—I have goals.”

Dreams are what you spend hours thinking about, and goals are what you work tirelessly to achieve. How do you set the right kind of powerful, challenging goals? One great formula is that of SMART goals, which says that goals should be Specific, Measurable, Attainable, Realistic, and Time-Bound.

As an example, we set a goal in May to double the amount of “Are You a Qwizard?” ebook downloads, and then rolled out some new strategies to promote it. We ended up with more than a 295% increase in May, so we learned a lot about what was realistic.

“I don’t get lucky. I make my own luck.”

The man makes his own luck, ladies and gentlemen. Spectacular.

But that’s not altogether impossible. In sports, luck is thrown around a lot. “That shot was lucky” or “They got lucky” or “That was a lucky break.” Well, a lot of hard work and preparation goes into getting to the point where luck can enter into the equation.

The Golden State Warriors hadn’t done anything much more than yawn-worthy in more than 20 years. But try and tell Steph Curry that their NBA title was lucky.

Suits doesn’t usually do much more than allude to the hours of research and discovery that goes into their cases (if they showed it, it would be more like CSPAN, and we nobody would watch it). While it’s true that you can catch a lucky break from time to time, if you’re relying on those moments to carry your marketing performance, you’re not going to last long.

“The only time ‘success’ comes before ‘work’ is in the dictionary.”

See above.

Man, Harvey can say pretty much the same thing and it still sounds so cool.

“Anyone can do my job, but no one can be me.”

Be irreplaceable. There are more than a million people in the US with the exact same degree, but only Harvey can be Harvey. Again, I’m aware that it’s a fictional program—but you and I both know people that have the same degree or experience that we do, but have been able to parlay that into mountainous success.

Look at this from a branding perspective, whether that’s from a corporate or personal standpoint. Being human is a good thing, and being yourself is even better. Brands that successfully humanize their voice enjoy more success on social media, and that translates into improved performance overall. Moz has a phenomenal guide to humanizing your brand.

There you go. There’s no limit to what these Harvey Specter quotes can help you achieve. You now have an unbreakable excuse to watch Suits at work :).

As software engineers, our natural habitat tends to be a dimly lit nerd cave.

Most of us focus on the beauty of our code, the health of our servers, and fighting to keep up with the latest and greatest technologies.

Very rarely do we take a software engineer and put them face to face with our customers—and why not?!

It’s likely due to one of these reasons:

  1. It’s uncomfortable
  2. It’s hard
  3. We’re too busy

It is so easy to zero in on the problems you are attempting to solve that you often forget the audience you’re attempting to solve the problem for. This needs to change.

Ditch the routine

Recently, I had the opportunity to tag along with the marketing team to the Social Media Week conference in Los Angeles. To be honest, I had some serious hesitation about subjecting myself to that level of human contact, but the experience ended up being phenomenal. Here are a few insights I took away from the experience:

Marketing and Sales reps aren’t heartless, greedy monsters

One of the first things that really struck me was how dedicated and amazing our marketing and sales teams are. In many organizations, sales and engineering are opposing forces, constantly struggling against one another for their own self interests. It was eye opening to see the genuine care they had for customers (existing and potential) and the desire to see them be successful on our platform. Of course sales has goals, but transactional metrics felt like secondary concerns when compared to making sure our customers were happy with the product we were providing.

You can’t top facetime with customers

Before the conference began, we had the opportunity to sit down with a few existing and potential customers to get feedback and brainstorm about incorporating quizzes and polls into their content strategies. These meetings were incredibly eye-opening. It was invigorating to hear success stories, as well as their challenges and struggles. In some cases, solving their pain required relatively little effort, but had it been brought back to the software engineering team, that message would likely have been lost in translation.

Engineers ride in elevators, too

While manning our booth, I was asked dozens of times to quickly describe what Qzzr is all about. There is something very humbling about practicing your elevator pitch. You really have to cut through the fluff and help someone understand your vision in as few words as possible. I struggled with this for a bit, and while I don’t think I ever polished my delivery as well as a standard sales team member, it did help me understand what our potential customers were most excited about.

Takeaway

This experience may not be for everyone, but we are adopting a rotation to allow our devs the opportunity to get out of the office for something like this. I have read posts from a variety of respectable people about getting out of the office, though I’m not sure any were directed at software engineers specifically. I can attest that it is valuable and encourage others to give it a go. Have you had any experiences with this? Share your insights in the comments below.

The problem arises in producing the kind of content that will resonate with an increasingly discerning public. There’s this weird phenomenon going on, where people are becoming more burned out with poorly produced “fluff” content, while searching ravenously for well-written, topical content.

The 80/20 rule

As a general rule, around 20% of your content will drive 80% of your traffic.

But why is this? What makes those posts so alluring? And how can you write your other posts in that vein?

  • Which traffic converts the best?
  • Are certain types of visitors more engaged than others? Why?
  • Does engagement yield more conversions/leads?
  • What can I do to improve engagement?
  • Where should I invest resources for more traffic?

]]> If your best content falls in the woods, does it have any value?

It does you little good to create the best content on Earth if no one is going to find it. So we wanted to come up with a go-to promotion strategy for marketing quizzes and other forms of interactive content. 

We’ve taught you how to create great content, how to find images for it, and even how to use it to take over the world. So what’s the next step? Spreading the word, obviously. You’re smart, you know the basics—stick it on Facebook and Twitter, put it on your blog, send it out through your newsletter. But what about the secrets, the things that set masters apart from amateurs? Well, that’s what we’re here for. We’ve picked up a few promotion strategy tricks along the way, and would love to share them with you.

First, a disclaimer: depending on who your customer or market is, your promotion strategy will be different. Not everything in this post will work for everyone, so instead of offering a to-do list, we offer this list as a toolbox, letting you choose your own tools for the job at hand.

Now that that’s over, let us introduce you to your new go-to resources for content promotion strategy.

Have awesome content

The first step, albeit obvious, must be stated: promoting content rests on the prerequisite of having content that’s worth promoting. What makes that? Good ideas, eye-catching headlines, captivating images. You’re already salivating just thinking about it, we know. And if you’re planning on using a quiz or two, check out our tips on how to make it sweet.

Use who you know

Take advantage of the people that already know you and like you. Not in a bad way, more in a you-liked-that-one-thing-so-let-me-show-you-something-kind-of-like-it type of way. This means any way your brand has of reaching customers, like social media, newsletters, and blogs.

Anywhere you have an audience that’s already shown they’re interested in what you have to say, put your content there! Since newsletter subscribers and blog viewers are fairly self-explanatory, we’ll focus mainly on how to reach your social followers. It doesn’t matter which social media platform you use most—whether it’s Facebook, Twitter, Instagram, YouTube, Google+, or Pinterest—something here applies.

Post it!

Once it goes live, make sure it goes out. On sites that allow it, use hashtags that work well in your industry; you can find them using sites like hashtagify.me and check their effectiveness with Rite Tag. Tag the inspiration for your content or anyone you feature (we’ll talk more about this later).

Post it again…and again

When the internet gets over 9,000 new tweets and 2,000 new Instagram photos per second, how do you get your posts noticed? The easy solution is to post more. Here’s a few tips straight out of the Qzzr playbook:

Twitter: On publish, 1 hour, 3 hours, 1 day, 3 days, 1 week, 1 month, 2 months
Facebook: On publish, 3 days, 1 week
LinkedIn: On publish, 1 week
Google Plus: On publish, 1 week

We know what you’re thinking though: what if your followers get annoyed? Guy Kawasaki doesn’t think so—in a recent webinar we did with him, he asserted that the potential for a few lost followers is well worth the risk.

However, facing that risk head-on may be more than you can handle. One trick is to present something different with each post, a tidbit that educates anyone who reads it, whether or not they click on your link. Never post the exact same thing twice. A few ways to introduce that variety:

Tweet the title

  • 6 Steps to a Sweet Quiz (and How to Make it Viral)

Pose a question

  • Of all the forms of content out there, why should you use quizzes and how do you make a killer one?

Quote an author

  • How to make it viral: get five influencers with a retweet ratio of 2 or higher to share it.

Cite a statistic

  • Did you know: The average quiz gets 1,900 shares and 84% of those shares are on Facebook.

Mention influencer

  • BuzzSumo’s Stephen Walsh paired up with Qzzr to talk quiz tactics, analytics, and stats.

Make a bold, attention-grabbing statement (that would double as a title)

  • Don’t fall behind! How to join the content revolution with quizzes.

Post a link

Post a photo

Time it right

But what’s the use of posting and posting again if no one sees it? You have to post when the most people are online.

To schedule, use something like Hootsuite or TweetDeck. For ideas about optimal posting times, here’s what we’ve picked up in our adventures:

  • More posts/day = more engagement
  • Most blog post shares happen on Thursday
  • For the most Facebook clicks, post at 1:00pm or 3:00pm on Thursday or Friday—for the most shares, post Saturday afternoon
  • For Twitter, 5:00pm weekdays or anytime weekends is best

Pin it

You’re probably thinking Pinterest, but you can pin posts in Twitter too (in Facebook, it’s called highlighting). This means anyone who looks at your profile will see these posts first.

Feature your image

Pinterest & Instagram are image-based websites, but that doesn’t mean you should neglect photos on your other accounts. Facebook thinks posts with photos are more “important,” which makes them more likely to appear in newsfeeds, and a tweet with an image is much more noticeable than all-text.

We like using stock photos under the Creative Commons license (you can find photos at these websites) and then editing them using Canva.

Post in groups

If your business is B2B or if content is directed to a very niche community, posting to groups is essential. Find Facebook groups, LinkedIn groups, and Google+ communities whose members would benefit from what you’re posting. Here at Qzzr, we usually try to find two or three groups on each website that are relevant.

Expand your reach

Last but not least, you can always draw more eyes by paying for views, if you’re so inclined. The great thing about this is the control you have over who you’re targeting—people who are similar to your current audience, age range, demographic, etc.

Find people who want to know you

If you have valuable content (and of course you do), it’ll mean something to others as well. So your job is to seek out these others and let them spread your words and images to other people in the industry as well.

Bloggers & influencers

Find the bloggers and influencers who write about your topic and find your content valuable. But remember: someone needs a reason to read, retweet, or recommend whatever it is you send them—and sometimes, they even need a reason to open the email you send them. As Alex Turnbull, the founder of Groove, quotes, “No one cares about your content unless they care about you.”

It’s a bit of a catch-22: how do you make people care about your content if you don’t email them? But why would they open your emails if they don’t care about you? The secret is to start building the relationship and the conversation before you need it. Start interacting with influencers by commenting thoughtfully on their posts, retweeting their content, and then emailing them personally after they’ve seen your name and responded a few times. It’s time consuming but it’s definitely worth it.

You can find bloggers or influencers (people with big followings who share content similar to yours) through any of several sites. Some focus on different social platforms, and others, like BuzzSumo, are multi-purpose—you can find bloggers and Twitter influencers.

Link roundups

Link roundups are another way to get your content out there. Many bloggers send out daily or weekly newsletters with a list of their favorite articles or links around the web. For example, Mattermark and Foundcy do this with startup-related content. Google something like “link roundup” or “weekly link roundup” along with a category name, and email the organizer.

Forums and submissions

Submit post to relevant chains. Find a reddit that’s similar to what you do. Or, if you’re in a very popular or large industry like startups or tech, you can submit to HackerNews for tech or Inbound.org and Growth Hackers for marketing. Forums are also a good niche place to send your content to—just find ones that apply to you and are made up of audiences that would find value in what you post. And just like with bloggers and influencers, it’s best to have a good standing within the group before you start posting.

Put yourself out there

Curation and aggregation get confused a lot. The easiest way to distinguish the two? Aggregation is usually automatic or, at least, not filtered based on any standard of quality, just amassed by keywords. Curated content is manually selected by someone and usually republished by that person with fresh insights, a new headline, or personal commentary. In other words, aggregation is collecting, while curation is choosing what’s in that collection. Both are great for submitting to to expose a wider audience to what you’re writing or making.

]]> Social media quizzes are a great way to engage with your audience in a personal and direct way, but not all quizzes are created equal. Just as any content can be created impeccably, there are a few common mistakes many quiz creators make that ruin their engagement results.

The secrets to reaching social media quiz sharing success are not difficult, but are often overlooked, causing numerous brands to miss out on growing their audience to new, wanting members.

Don’t trip into these social media quiz pitfalls!

1. You don’t make your results shareable

If you’re looking for a way to really educate your audience about your product, a quiz may not be the most in-depth solution. Quizzes are not the best format for informational posts—at least in this medium. Instead, use a social media quiz to educate users about your brand in a short, fun and conversational way.

Quizzes are great for brand awareness. For them to be effective, they should be easy to share. When the quiz taker gets their results, they need an immediate way to post to Facebook, Twitter, and other social media platforms. The general consensus is that the format should show the results first and then the title of the quiz second. For example: “You’re Princess Jasmine! – Which Disney Princess Are You? Take the quiz here to find out!”

2. Your quiz is too long

With the average attention span at an alltime low, you have just a few short seconds to engage your audience with useful, entertaining content before they move on to the next shiny thing.

The most successful quizzes average between seven and ten questions. Keep your questions succinct and to the point; big blocks of text are sure to yield fewer results than pithy inquiries. And keep your results short as well. It is a delicate balance, however. You definitely want people taking social media quizzes to feel like they’re receiving quality feedback at the end of the quiz, so dip much less than seven and your credibility will suffer. But, as a general rule, wordy explanations that take longer to read result in fewer shares.

3. You don’t use enough (good) images

Make up for less text by using quality images. The old saying, “a picture is worth a thousand words,” holds true in quizzes as much as anything else.

If you’re going to spend a lot of time on any aspect of the quiz you’re creating, spend it on the most focal image. The main image seen is what draws users to your quiz in the first place.

Images historically receive more shares on social media than text, and a quiz post is made up of 80% image. Ultimately, the photo replaces the “lede sentence” that hooks the audience in a typical text post.

Not just any cool image will do here. Your lede photo should be relevant to your content, as well as high quality. A low resolution, pixelated image will be scrolled over by most viewers. Studies also show that images with faces tend to do better than photos without. The human brain is programed to look for other humans. These images are familiar and relatable, which makes them more clickable and shareable.

Images don’t just apply to the introduction to your quiz, either. High quality photos should be sprinkled throughout. Use images as questions or answers in your quiz, and especially to demonstrate the results.

4. Your title sucks

A close second to finding a great lede image is creating a catchy title. Think of it as the image drawing their attention, but the title giving the audience a reason to click. In fact, research shows that 80% of post clicks are motivated by a headline alone.

Your title should stick out in the massive content mill of social media, so here are a few tried and true tricks to make your social media quiz stand out:

  • Favorite the Famous – Studies show that people are more likely to click on a title with a celebrity’s name in it than one without. Why? We like to see ourselves as celebrities and we relate to famous things. Who wouldn’t want to be like Beyonce or doesn’t see a bit of their own friend group in the “Friends” cast?
  • Rate Users – These headings are best used for personality quizzes. Titles like, “Which Television Anti-hero Are You?” give you a creative way to rate and analyze people, which users then want to share with their friends.
  • The Power of “Actually” – You can easily turn a boring title into something provocative by throwing in the word “actually.” Instead of asking, “What’s Your Sense of Humor?” entice users by saying, “Do You Actually Have a Sense of Humor?”

5. Your quiz is depressing

Users don’t take a social media quiz to feel badly about themselves. Boost your quiz shareability with positive results that make people excited to brag about it on social media.

It might seem like a controversial result technique will be provocative enough to get shares, but the research shows otherwise.

Don’t bum users out with negative feedback, but don’t lie either. Results that are too broad or overly positive feel like they’re pandering, and people are less likely to share those with friends. Instead, focus your questions and results around uplifting verbiage and honest answers.

6. Your quiz is a trap

And not in a good way.

This goes back to the purpose of using quizzes in your marketing strategy. The goal of quizzes isn’t always to get leads, but also to create educational and entertaining ways to spread brand awareness. You don’t necessarily need to use quizzes to create an email newsletter mailing list, but instead could use them as a fun format designed for shareability about your brand. Pair the message with the intended result, and compare that to where the audience is in respect to your funnel.

You can generate leads from a social media quiz, but this is done through providing personalized recommendations, not blackmailing quiz users into giving you their information. If your quiz can help users determine the type of product you offer that most suits their needs, they may be motivated to subscribe to your newsletters or purchase a product. But that decision should be the user’s initiative, not yours.

Trapping people into giving you personal information before showing results will lead most users to opt-out of your quiz all together, which means you don’t get to show them more information about your brand, lift their spirits, or get social media shares that drive more traffic. When people feel like they’re given something that was tailored for them, they’re much more likely to opt-in, as opposed to just being blindly marketed at.

7. Your quiz is boring

Buzzfeed, one of the most successful websites using quiz-based traffic generation, keeps their audience clicking with engaging content, both visually and integrally. Big, colorful fonts and lots of images keep the user entertained aesthetically while the conversational, witty content engages their mind.

Avoid using quizzes as methods for hardcore learning, and instead don’t be afraid to be funny, and even snarky in your quiz content. Studies show that using personal pronouns, like “I,” “we” and “you,” create a more conversational, personalized tone that prompts readers to view and share your quiz.

This especially applies to the results of your quiz. Users want to feel like their results are unique and specially tailored just for them, so write your results as if you’re writing to a single person, not a large audience. Try picturing a friend that matches the answers you’re writing and create content directly to them. It will come out more casual and connected than writing to an imaginary audience, which can seem formulaic.

Conclusion

Become a quiz-creating guru by inserting these simple steps into your everyday process. Orchestrating outstanding quizzes that engage your audience and build your brand is an attainable skill if you avoid these common quiz mistakes.
